📖 What is a market maker? ▸
Think of a market maker like a currency exchange booth at an airport. They always quote two prices: one to buy from you, one to sell to you. The gap between those two prices — called the spread — is their revenue.

The danger: if they buy too much and prices fall, they're stuck holding losses. This is called inventory risk. Our engine solves this using a 2008 academic paper by Avellaneda & Stoikov.
📚 How the order book works ▸
The Limit Order Book (LOB) is a live list of every pending buy and sell order, sorted by price.

Green rows (bids) = people waiting to buy. Red rows (asks) = people waiting to sell. When a new order matches an existing one, a trade fires and both disappear from the book. We post our own quotes into this book.
🧮 Avellaneda-Stoikov model ▸
The A-S paper (2008) answered: "Where exactly should I place my quotes?"

Reservation price r = we shift our "fair value" away from mid depending on how much inventory we hold. Long positions → shade price down to attract sellers.

Optimal spread δ* = two components. First: inventory risk (shrinks as session ends). Second: protection against informed traders (stays fixed).
r = s - q·γ·σ²·(T-t)
δ* = γσ²(T-t) + (2/γ)·ln(1 + γ/κ)
🤖 What the RL agent learned ▸
We trained a PPO neural network over 30 simulated sessions. It learns to answer: "Should I tighten or widen my spread right now?"

It sees 11 inputs: inventory, VPIN, imbalance, spread, volatility, time left, P&L, drawdown, depth.

Result: the agent chose γ×2.0 in 64.9% of ticks — it learned that being cautious about inventory beats chasing fills. Reward improved +1.94 over training.
☠️ VPIN — toxicity detector ▸
VPIN detects whether informed traders (people who know something you don't) are active.

If someone keeps hitting our bids and never touches our asks, they probably know the price is about to fall. VPIN tracks this imbalance. Above 0.65 → we stop quoting entirely. Better to earn nothing than get picked off by someone smarter.
